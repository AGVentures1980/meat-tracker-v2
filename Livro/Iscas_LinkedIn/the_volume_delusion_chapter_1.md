## Chapter 1: The Journey and the Market Diagnosis

My journey in the gastronomic sector began at the bottom of the trenches, humbly, but fully immersed in high-volume operations. My first job was as a meat carver at **Texas de Brazil** in Orlando, Florida. During my two years there, I experienced the absolute essence of customer service and understood that a restaurant's experience goes far beyond what is served at the table—it is dictated by the millimeter precision behind the scenes.

My evolution was rapid: I started working with the brand's expansion, helping to structure and open new units across the USA. Just a year later, I was invited to take on my first management position in Miami Beach, where I spent three years executing operations with extremely high demands. Each experience brought a new set of challenges, exposing me to operations where any 1% failure in protein cost (COGS) represented hundreds of thousands of dollars lost by the end of the year.

Back in Brazil, I began a new executive phase at **Fogo de Chão**, where, in just eight months, I was promoted to Service Manager. I was immersed in corporate expansion projects and excellence auditing across all units. However, the pandemic drastically changed the global landscape, forcing me to recalibrate and rebuild operations in Santa Catarina.

Even amidst the greatest crisis in the history of the industry, I found new opportunities for matrix optimization at the Noma Group and Pecorino. Managing these disparate operations led me to identify the pattern: **The Silent Disease of Scale**. 

These experiences shaped my operational combat philosophy. I discovered that profit isn't lost on the dining room floor; it evaporates in the "blind spots" between the receiving dock and the prep table. And that managing people, armed with the correct technology, is the only shield against "Ghost Math". People create the experience. But it is the invisible numbers and the absolute control of your chain that ensure the doors stay open tomorrow.
